***How to Check In With Yourself and Stay on Track***

Self-monitoring is the executive function skill that allows us to step back, reflect, and adjust in the moment. It’s like having an internal coach who helps you notice: *Am I on the right track? Do I need to change something? Am I staying focused on the goal? How can I make changes to make this more efficient?*

This is not an easy task for many. Without self-monitoring, people may rush through tasks without realizing they’ve missed important steps, or they may keep repeating the same mistake without noticing. In conversations, weak self-monitoring can lead to interrupting (which I’ll talk more about in another blog so be on the lookout), dominating the discussion, or missing social cues. Specifically conversations, lack of self monitoring can lead to communication breakdowns, potential for loss of connection and loss of confidence.

In speech therapy, self-monitoring strategies often include:

- **Checklists and rubrics**: creating specific criteria to compare progress against.
- **Self-reflection prompts**: asking “How do I know I’m finished?” or “Did this go the way I wanted it to?”
- **Recording and replaying**: reviewing work or speech to notice patterns.
- **Mindfulness techniques**: building awareness of thoughts, emotions, and behaviors in the moment.

Self-monitoring helps us pause before reacting, catch errors before they escalate, and make better decisions. For students, this might mean reviewing homework before turning it in. For professionals, it could mean noticing when a presentation is going off track and course-correcting. For anyone, it improves communication, relationships, and personal growth.

The ability to self-monitor doesn’t develop overnight. It requires consistent practice and supportive feedback. But with time, it becomes second nature—a skill that empowers independence, accountability, and success.
