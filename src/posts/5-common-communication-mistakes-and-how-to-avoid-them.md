We’ve all been in situations where we wanted to say something. We knew WHAT we wanted to say. We knew WHY we wanted to say it. We even might have known HOW we wanted to say it. But at that moment, we simply couldn’t convey the message accurately. Here are five mistakes that are commonly made, and how to avoid them. If we stay present in the moment, and remain self-aware, we can really cut back on communication errors.

**1. Listening to Reply Instead of Listening to Understand**

When we’re focused on what *we’ll* say next, we often miss the real point of what the other person is saying. We need to be watching body language, listening to tone of voice, and assessing the nonverbal cues we’re being given. Jumping in can be tempting at times but rarely leads to a solid communication exchange.
**Fix:** Slow down, stay present, and let them fully finish before responding.

**2. Assuming Instead of Asking**

Jumping to conclusions can lead to misunderstandings and unnecessary conflict. When we make assumptions about what the other person knows, what their motives may be, or how they might perceive a situation, we leave ourselves wide open for communication breakdown, that could have been avoided if all the information was available.
**Fix:** Ask clarifying questions before reacting or making a decision.

**3. Using Too Much Jargon**

Big words and technical terms can alienate or confuse listeners. If you’re going to use big words, consider including the definition in the following sentence.
**Fix:** Use clear, accessible language—especially with people outside your field.

**4. Multitasking While Talking**

Trying to text, check emails, or think about your to-do list sends the message, “I’m not really listening.” This can lead to frustration for your communication partner. If they’re speaking to you, they want to be heard. We all know how it feels when we have to repeat ourselves.
**Fix:** Give your full attention—it shows respect and builds trust.

**5. Not Matching Your Nonverbal Cues to Your Words**

If your tone, body language, and facial expressions don’t align with your message, people may not believe what you’re saying.
**Fix:** Be aware of your nonverbal signals and make sure they support your words.

To put it simply, good communication isn’t about saying more—it’s about saying the right things, in the right way, at the right time. Using the strategies above, can maximize your communication effectiveness in any situation.
